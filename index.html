This project leverages the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) dataset to visualize average home purchase loan values in New York City by census tract for 2018 and 2023, and the change in average home purchase loan values between the two years.

Click locations on the map and change views with Map Options, or read below, to explore the map

Urban theorists and academics have shown the importance of understanding the movement of financial value in and around cities. If there is too little value in a neighborhood, it suffers from disinvestment and marginalization [1]. On the other hand, too much financial value can destroy cohesive neghborhoods [2], accelerate displacement [3], and exploit collective social resources [4].

Given these stakes, it is critical to develop ways to understand the movement of financial capital. Visualizing home purchase loan values are one way to do this. As measures of the debt that property owners are willing to take on to invest or live in a neighborhood, loan values can intuitively show where value is pooling or draining.

In the map, high loan value census tracts seem to be relatively dispersed across New York City. There is a cluster of high loan values areas around Crown Heights, Brooklyn.

In the map, high loan values seem to migrate to Manhattan and the East River waterfront in Brooklyn. The Upper West Side stands out as a prominent cluster of high loan value areas.

In the map, the height of the blocks represent the difference between the two years in absolute value terms. A high block means that the average loan value in the census tract in 2023 was much higher than it was in 2018. This view of the data helps depict the sheer difference in loan values across New York City.
